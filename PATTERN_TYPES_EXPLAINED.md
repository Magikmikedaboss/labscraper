# What Are Pattern Types? (Simple Explanation)

## The Core Idea

**A pattern type describes how research behaves over time around a topic.**

It's NOT about:
- Whether something works
- Whether it's safe
- Whether it's proven

It's about:
- Where researchers are focusing effort
- How that effort is changing
- What trajectory the research is following

---

## Think of It Like This

Imagine you're watching a group of scientists work on different problems over several years. You notice they behave in predictable ways:

### 🟢 **Convergence Pattern**
**What you see:** More and more researchers using the same approach

**Real example from your data:**
- LC-MS (mass spectrometry) appears in 85 out of 647 events
- Everyone keeps using it
- It's becoming the standard method

**What this tells you:**
"Researchers are agreeing this is a good way to measure things"

**What this does NOT tell you:**
Whether LC-MS is "the best" method (just that it's popular)

---

### 🔵 **Escalation Pattern**
**What you see:** Research moving from simple tests to harder tests

**Real example:**
- Year 1: Testing peptide in a test tube (in vitro)
- Year 2: Testing peptide in mice (in vivo)
- Year 3: Testing peptide in human blood samples

**What this tells you:**
"Researchers think this is promising enough to test in harder conditions"

**What this does NOT tell you:**
Whether it will work in humans (just that they're trying)

---

### 🟠 **Stagnation Pattern**
**What you see:** Same problem keeps coming up, no new solutions

**Real example from your data:**
- AGGREGATION appears in 24 events
- Papers keep saying "the peptide clumps together"
- Same problem, different papers, no breakthrough

**What this tells you:**
"This is a persistent technical challenge researchers keep hitting"

**What this does NOT tell you:**
That it's impossible to solve (just that it hasn't been solved yet)

---

### 🔴 **Abandonment Pattern**
**What you see:** Researchers try something briefly, then stop

**Real example:**
- 2020: 5 papers about compound X
- 2021: 2 papers about compound X
- 2022: 0 papers about compound X

**What this tells you:**
"Research attention has moved away from this"

**What this does NOT tell you:**
Why they stopped (could be funding, could be results, could be anything)

---

### 🟣 **Fragmentation Pattern**
**What you see:** Everyone using different methods, no agreement

**Real example from your data:**
- 24 different assay types being used
- No single method dominates
- Low recurrence across all methods

**What this tells you:**
"The field is still figuring out the best way to study this"

**What this does NOT tell you:**
That the field is confused or failing (just that it's early/exploratory)

---

## Why This Matters

### Traditional approach:
❌ Read 100 papers  
❌ Try to figure out "what works"  
❌ Get overwhelmed by contradictions  
❌ Make a guess  

### Pattern approach:
✅ Look at how research **behaves** across 100 papers  
✅ See where effort is **clustering** (Convergence)  
✅ See where effort is **progressing** (Escalation)  
✅ See where effort is **stuck** (Stagnation)  
✅ See where effort is **declining** (Abandonment)  
✅ See where effort is **scattered** (Fragmentation)  

**You get insight without making claims about "truth".**

**What pattern type is this?**

- SEMAGLUTIDE appears in 10 events
- Same methods being used (Convergence)
- Moving from cell tests to animal tests (Escalation)
- Papers say "effective", "improved", "robust" (Positive signals)
- Frequency increasing over time (Momentum)

**Pattern Type:** Convergence + Escalation

**What this tells you:**
"Research effort is clustering around SEMAGLUTIDE with increasing validation efforts and predominantly positive outcome language."

**What this does NOT tell you:**
- That SEMAGLUTIDE definitely works
- That it's safe for everyone
- That you should use it

**But it DOES tell you:**
- Researchers think it's worth studying
- They're investing more effort over time
- They're moving it toward harder tests
- They're reporting positive results

**That's useful information without overclaiming.**

## Another Real Example: AGGREGATION

**What pattern type is this?**

Looking at your 647 events:
- AGGREGATION appears in 24 events
- Same problem keeps appearing (Stagnation)
- Papers say "aggregated", "unstable", "poor stability" (Negative signals)
- No new solutions being introduced
- Frequency stable (not increasing or decreasing)

**Pattern Type:** Stagnation

**What this tells you:**
"Aggregation appears as a persistent technical barrier with predominantly negative outcome language and limited reported progress."

**What this does NOT tell you:**
- That aggregation is impossible to solve
- That researchers should give up
- That all peptides aggregate

**But it DOES tell you:**
- This is a recurring challenge
- Current approaches aren't solving it
- Researchers keep encountering it
- It's a known friction point

**That's honest and useful.**

## How You Use Pattern Types

### Question: "Should I try peptide therapy for diabetes?"

**Bad answer (overclaiming):**
"Yes, SEMAGLUTIDE works and is safe."

**Good answer (pattern-based):**
"SEMAGLUTIDE shows strong research momentum with convergence around consistent methods and predominantly positive outcome language. However, aggregation and stability remain persistent technical challenges across peptide research."

**This gives you:**
- What's getting attention (SEMAGLUTIDE)
- What trajectory it's on (Convergence + Escalation)
- What challenges exist (Aggregation = Stagnation)
- Honest language (momentum, not effectiveness)

**You can make an informed decision without being lied to.**

---

## Summary

**Pattern Types are:**
- How research **behaves** over time
- Observable across multiple papers
- Descriptive, not prescriptive
- Honest about uncertainty

**Pattern Types are NOT:**
- Claims about what works
- Recommendations
- Biological truth
- Medical advice

**They're a way to see the forest (research trajectory) instead of just trees (individual papers).**

## Does This Make Sense?

Pattern types are just a way to describe **how researchers behave** when studying something.

- Are they all using the same method? (Convergence)
- Are they moving to harder tests? (Escalation)
- Are they stuck on the same problem? (Stagnation)
- Are they losing interest? (Abandonment)
- Are they all over the place? (Fragmentation)

**That's it. Simple behavioral observation.**

**No claims. No hype. Just patterns.**
